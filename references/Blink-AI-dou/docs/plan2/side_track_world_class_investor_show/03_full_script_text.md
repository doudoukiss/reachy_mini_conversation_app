# Full Script Text

This file is the clean spoken script for the first version of the investor show.

Use it for:
- voice rehearsal,
- caption authoring,
- local TTS generation,
- and cue-by-cue timing checks.

## Role legend

- **ROBOT** = investor-facing narration spoken by Blink-AI
- **OFF-STAGE PROMPT** = prewritten prompt spoken off-stage or shown as caption
- **SCREEN** = optional chapter caption or key phrase on the main display

---

## Chapter 1 — Opening Reveal

**SCREEN:** `Blink-AI — Local-first companion. Optional embodiment.`

**ROBOT:**  
Good morning. I’m Blink-AI.  

What you are seeing is not a separate robot mind. It is the same local-first companion runtime living on the nearby computer, projected into this head when embodiment is useful.  

In the next ten minutes, I will show you six things that matter in a real company: presence, grounded understanding, memory, useful operation, safe oversight, and replayable proof.

---

## Chapter 2 — Same Mind, Optional Embodiment

**SCREEN:** `One companion. Multiple projections.`

**ROBOT:**  
My motions are not hand-authored servo theater.  

The runtime resolves states like attention, listening, thinking, and safe idle. The embodiment layer then compiles those states into controlled, servo-safe motion.  

That distinction matters. The product is not a robot-first stunt. The product is the companion. The head is one optional way that companion can show up in the world.  

That is why the path from today's demo to a credible deployment is disciplined. The mind stays consistent even when the surface changes.

---

## Chapter 3 — Grounded Perception

**SCREEN:** `Grounded perception`

**OFF-STAGE PROMPT:**  
Blink, what sign can you see right now?

**ROBOT:**  
The visible sign says Workshop Room, with the direction pointing to the right.  

That answer is grounded in the current scene record, not pulled from a canned script.  

And the important part is not that I answered correctly once. The important part is that the answer is traceable. If the scene were stale, limited, or uncertain, I would say that plainly instead of pretending to know.

---

## Chapter 4 — Memory And Continuity

**SCREEN:** `Continuity without fake intimacy`

**OFF-STAGE PROMPT:**  
Hi, I’m Alex. I prefer the quiet route. Where is the quiet room?

**ROBOT:**  
Welcome, Alex. I can guide you to the quiet room using the quieter route.  

**OFF-STAGE PROMPT:**  
What do you remember about me?

**ROBOT:**  
You introduced yourself as Alex, and you prefer the quiet route.  

That kind of continuity is what turns a demo into a product. It means the interaction can pick up where it left off, stay useful, and remain legible to the operator instead of disappearing into a hidden prompt state.

---

## Chapter 5 — Useful Concierge Behavior

**SCREEN:** `Commercially believable utility`

**OFF-STAGE PROMPT:**  
What time does the Robotics Workshop start?

**ROBOT:**  
The Robotics Workshop starts at 6:00 PM in the Workshop Room.  

That may sound simple, but simplicity is the point. In a community center, library branch, clinic lobby, or guided venue, useful grounded help is what earns trust first.  

A believable product does not begin with maximum autonomy. It begins by being reliably helpful in the moments people actually care about.

---

## Chapter 6 — Operator Oversight

**SCREEN:** `Operator oversight and handoff`

**OFF-STAGE PROMPT:**  
I need the accessible route and a staff member to help me.

**ROBOT:**  
I can guide you to the accessible route, and I am opening a staff assist handoff now.  

This is one of the most important parts of the system. Real deployment means knowing when autonomy is enough, when a human should enter the loop, and how to make that handoff visible instead of ambiguous.  

On the operator side, this becomes a concrete ticket, a status timeline, and a recommended next step. That is not a fallback to hide. That is operational maturity.

---

## Chapter 7 — Honest Fallback And Safe Idle

**SCREEN:** `Safe fallback`

**ROBOT:**  
Now I will show the opposite of a stunt.  

When transport degrades or the runtime decides conditions are no longer safe, I switch to safe idle. I become more predictable, not more improvisational.  

That protects hardware, protects trust, and makes the state visible to the operator. In a real deployment, honesty under degraded conditions matters more than a flashy recovery line.

---

## Chapter 8 — Evidence, Replayability, And Close

**SCREEN:** `Replayable evidence`

**ROBOT:**  
Every serious demo should leave evidence behind.  

This run produces traces, command acknowledgements, grounding sources, incident records, and an exportable artifact bundle. That means the team can review what happened, compare runs, score regressions, and improve the product without turning every demo into folklore.  

So the real takeaway is not that I can move, or speak, or answer one question. The real takeaway is that the same local-first companion can live on a nearby computer, become useful in real settings, project into optional embodiment when presence helps, and remain safe, inspectable, and deployable.  

That is a credible product direction.  

Thank you.
